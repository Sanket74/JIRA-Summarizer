1
User problem
Your version
User is manually searching over Jira by reading descriptions to check what has been done and finding similar tickets where resolution is mentioned.
→
Refined
Support managers and engineers scan 20–30k Jira tickets daily to triage issues, understand current status, and find similar resolved tickets for reference. Reading full descriptions manually takes 30–40 min per session. The pain is not just reading — it is context-switching: a user must hold one ticket's detail in memory while searching for a related one. Success = a manager can triage the top 80 tickets of the day and surface 5 related tickets per issue in under 5 minutes, with no ticket opened.
Why this matters: your original version described the task but not the time cost or the specific user action that defines success. The module asks "what does success look like for them?" — that answer needs to be concrete and measurable, not directional.
2
AI fit assessment
Data availability
Partially
Raw ticket data exists (30k tickets). However, an eval dataset — 100–200 tickets with human-labelled "correct" summaries and verified related-ticket mappings — does not exist yet and must be created before building begins. Without it, "90% accuracy" cannot be measured. This is the most urgent pre-build task.
Error tolerance
Inconvenience → Time loss
Wrong summaries waste engineer time (they must open the ticket anyway). Wrong related-ticket mappings mean missed context on resolution paths. Neither is a financial or safety risk — but repeated errors cause tool abandonment, which is the real failure mode. The cost of wrong output is trust loss, not direct harm.
Action reversibility
Yes
The pipeline only reads and writes summaries. It does not modify any ticket, assign ownership, or send notifications. Wrong outputs can be regenerated by correcting the prompt or re-running the pipeline. No irreversible actions exist in this system.
Volume
Yes
480 summaries per daily run (80 primary + 400 related). At 20–30k total tickets, manual triage is not feasible. Volume clearly justifies automation.
Fix applied: "Partially" on data availability is more honest than "Yes" — you have the raw data but not the eval set. The module specifically calls out that eval data must exist before you can claim a threshold. You cannot say "90% accuracy" without first defining what correct looks like in a labelled dataset.

AI archetype
Archetype
Pipeline
Rationale
AI is a processing step with no real-time user interaction at runtime. Structured ticket data goes in; structured summaries and related-ticket mappings come out, feeding a navigation dashboard that humans browse.
Your original rationale ("not fully automated, part of the ecosystem") is close but technically describes Autopilot, not Pipeline. The defining Pipeline characteristic is: no human in the loop at runtime. The output feeds a system — a dashboard — and humans interact with that system, not with the AI directly. That one sentence changes how you spec everything downstream.

Scope boundary
Your version — critical error
Will do: Summarise Jira tickets

Will NOT do: similarity search for tickets based on summary
→
Refined
Will do:
· Read ticket fields (body, priority, queue, type, tags) and compress to 15–20 word summary
· Use shared tags to identify 5 related tickets per primary ticket
· Output structured record per ticket (ID, summary, word count, related IDs)

Will NOT do:
· Select which 80 tickets are "top tickets of the day" (upstream job)
· Assign, escalate, close or modify any ticket field
· Send notifications or emails
· Infer or add facts not present in the ticket
Your original scope had a logical error: "similarity search" was listed under Will NOT do — but finding related tickets IS the core job of this system (the 80×5 = 400 related summaries). This must be in the Will Do list. What it will NOT do is modify tickets or make autonomous decisions.
5
Probabilistic success definition
Your version — not testable
"90% correct summary with threshold of 5%"

Most important failure: mapping incorrect tickets
→
Refined — 3-part format from module
Three criteria, each with metric + threshold + condition.
Criterion 1 — Normal inputs
Summary quality score shall exceed 85% on a human-reviewed eval set of 200 tickets sampled from the last 90 days of production data.
Quality score = passes the 4-part checklist: who affected + what broke + urgency signal + current state, all present in ≤20 words.
Criterion 2 — Related ticket mapping
Related ticket accuracy shall exceed 80% on the same eval set, where accuracy = at least 3 of 5 linked tickets share a verified root cause or component with the primary ticket.
This is the most important failure mode: a wrong mapping is worse than a weak summary because it sends the engineer in the wrong direction.
Criterion 3 — Failure handling
Word count compliance shall be 100%: any summary outside 10–22 words is flagged and held from the dashboard pending review. Schema compliance (ticket_id + summary + related_ids present) shall be 100% per run.
These are programmatically checkable — no human eval needed. They are your safety net before summaries reach users.
Human oversight
Exception-based
Not Post-hoc. Post-hoc means AI acts and humans audit later — risky for trust. Exception-based means: summaries that pass schema and word count checks are auto-published; those that fail are held for human review. A 10% random sample is audited weekly to catch quality drift before users notice it.
6
Open questions before proceeding
Your 3 questions
1. Output format (only know 15–20 words)
2. Multiple tickets mapping to generic tickets
3. What type of data are we looking at?
→
Refined — sharper, decision-changing
Good instincts. Sharpened to be genuinely decision-changing per the module brief.
Q1
Who creates the eval dataset, and how long will it take?
If no one owns this task, the "90% accuracy" goal is unmeasurable and the build cannot be validated. This is the highest-risk unknown — it could delay the entire project.
Q2
How are "top 80 tickets of the day" selected — by volume, priority, recency, or a combination?
This is an upstream dependency. If the selection logic changes, the summary pipeline's input distribution changes, which can break accuracy benchmarks. The pipeline's scope boundary explicitly excludes this — but someone must own it.
Q3
What is the acceptable false-positive rate for related ticket linking — and who judges "related"?
Your instinct about "multiple tickets mapping to generic tickets" is correct. Some tickets (e.g. Outage + Account) will attract dozens of weak links. Without a human-defined threshold for what counts as a valid link, the eval set cannot be labelled and the 80% mapping accuracy criterion cannot be measured.
